\section*{What I Created and Who I Shared It With}

For my joy-oriented literacy pursuit, I treated my \textit{Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS framework} as an ongoing literacy project in action. The classroom lessons I facilitated became the project outputs: moments where students experienced literacy through building, collaboration, speaking, movement, problem-solving, and shared excitement. The strongest examples came from my Kindergarten squishy circuit lessons with airplanes, light bulbs, and motor-based fans, along with Minecraft lessons in Grades 3 and 4. These activities were shared with students and with the classroom community because students built, watched, helped, revised, and learned from one another in real time.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/joy-evidence-from-placement.md}{\textit{Placement evidence: six documented examples across Grade 4 and Kindergarten}}}

In Kindergarten, students learned about batteries, switches, wires, Play-Doh, conductors, insulators, and motor-based fans by watching, naming parts, holding materials, connecting pieces, and counting down together before the fan turned on. In the airplane-building lesson, students worked in teams, taking roles such as building wings, shaping the body, making wheels, and connecting the fan to a switch.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/kindergarten-circuit-demo-airplane-prep-evidence.md}{\textit{Kindergarten transcript evidence --- circuit demo and airplane prep}}} In Grade 4 Minecraft, students who finished helped classmates troubleshoot, turning digital literacy into peer teaching and community responsibility.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/grade-4-minecraft-agent-challenge-evidence.md}{\textit{Grade 4 transcript evidence --- Minecraft agent challenge}}}

Seen through a Universal Design for Learning (UDL) lens, each lesson became a different piece of the larger Puzzle Plan. The Kindergarten circuits emphasized multiple ways to engage and understand: students touched materials, heard vocabulary, watched the circuit work, and participated in the class countdown. The Minecraft lessons emphasized multiple ways to act and express understanding: students counted, coded, debugged, explained, and helped one another. This matters because the project was not only about creating fun lessons; it was about testing whether literacy becomes more joyful when students have more than one pathway into the work.

\section*{My Creative and Learning Process}

My creative process began with the question: \textit{how can technology lessons become more than task completion?} Knobel and Lankshear helped me name these moments as new literacies because students were making meaning through digital, visual, participatory, and networked practices. Price-Dennis, Holmes, and Smith helped me connect this to access because digital tools matter when they open more ways for students to participate. Brooks and Frankel also reminded me that students bring languages, cultures, histories, identities, and community knowledge into technology use.

In the squishy circuit lessons, I used switches, batteries, wires, Play-Doh, fans, and model-building pieces to connect vocabulary to action. Students did not only hear the word ``switch''; they saw it, held it, connected it, and watched what happened.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/kindergarten-circuit-demo-airplane-prep-evidence.md}{\textit{Evidence: Example 5 --- vocabulary and collective participation}}} In Minecraft, I treated students who finished early as class resources rather than treating completion as the end of learning. That shifted the lesson from ``I am done'' to ``I can help someone else succeed,'' allowing one student to troubleshoot while another deepened mastery through explanation.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/grade-4-minecraft-agent-challenge-evidence.md}{\textit{Evidence: Example 2 --- peer teaching as joyful expertise}}}

I also began to see the lessons as small design experiments. If a student could explain a circuit orally but not write a full explanation yet, that still counted as literacy. If a student could debug a Minecraft path by counting blocks and revising commands, that also counted as literacy. If a student could help a peer understand what to do next, that was evidence of communication, interpretation, and expertise. These moments helped me trust that literacy was already happening; my responsibility was to design the conditions where it could become visible.

This process taught me to notice joy as part of design, not as a bonus. Joy happened because students had access to materials, roles, language, peer support, and visible success. Pinkard's ecosystem view helped me name this: students need tools, mentors, audiences, feedback, and real reasons to create. Stornaiuolo and Thomas helped me see digital literacy as agency, where young people use tools to participate and organize meaning. My design principle became simple: \textit{students need enough structure to feel safe and enough freedom to feel ownership}.

\section*{How This Cultivated Joy, Love, and Aesthetic Fulfillment}

This pursuit cultivated joy because students saw their learning become real. In Kindergarten, the joy came from collective anticipation: students watched the pieces connect, counted down together, and saw the fan turn on.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/kindergarten-circuit-demo-airplane-prep-evidence.md}{\textit{Evidence: Example 5 --- collective countdown and fan circuit demo}}} The science became visible, audible, physical, and shared. The joy was not only that the fan worked; it was that the class experienced success together.

The lessons also created aesthetic fulfillment because students built objects with shape, motion, design, and purpose. Their work had form, creativity, effort, and a relationship to the physical world. Love appeared through relationships. In Minecraft, students who understood the task helped classmates who were still working. Success was not private or competitive; it became something students could give to each other.\footnote{\href{https://github.com/pzg8794/EDU498-joy_oriented_literacy_pursuit_journal/blob/main/grade-4-minecraft-agent-challenge-evidence.md}{\textit{Evidence: Example 2 --- peer teaching in the Minecraft session}}}

The love in this project came from how students learned with and for one another. In the circuit lesson, the class did not simply watch one student succeed; they helped the student succeed. In Minecraft, the student who finished early was not removed from the learning community; he became part of someone else's success. That changed the emotional structure of the classroom. Learning became less about individual performance and more about shared possibility.

These moments reminded me that joy in literacy does not always look quiet or neat. Sometimes it looks like trying again, arguing productively, watching a fan turn on, helping a peer, or realizing that one's hands, ideas, and voice matter. For me, aesthetic fulfillment is the moment learning feels alive and students can see themselves inside the work.

\section*{Muhammad's Literacy Pursuit and Digital Literacy Readings}

\textit{Muhammad's framework} helps me understand that a literacy pursuit should develop identity, skills, intellect, criticality, and joy together. The Kindergarten circuits and Minecraft lessons were not only science or technology activities. They helped students build vocabulary and technical understanding while seeing themselves as builders, helpers, problem-solvers, and contributors.

Knobel and Lankshear helped me name these practices as new literacies. Price-Dennis, Holmes, and Smith connected the work to inclusive digital literacy because students need more than one pathway to show understanding. Brooks and Frankel's work on transnational digital literacy matters because students bring identity and community knowledge into classroom technologies. When a student builds in Minecraft, explains a circuit orally, draws a design, or helps a peer troubleshoot, that student is practicing literacy.

Melo-Pfeifer and Gertz pushed me to think about criticality because digital and media spaces are not neutral. Students need to question images, missing voices, credibility, representation, and assumptions. Common Sense Digital Citizenship extends this through privacy, audience, confirmation bias, online identity, and community responsibility. Stornaiuolo and Thomas emphasize youth agency, while Pinkard reminds us that students need tools, mentors, audiences, feedback, and reasons to create. Serpagli and Mensah's work with social media-style science learning connects to my placement examples because students used active and shared forms to explain technical ideas. Together, these readings show that digital literacy should position students as creators, not just consumers.

\section*{How This Informs My Future Teaching}

This experience reminds me that literacy is not limited to written language. Literacy is also building, coding, designing, explaining, translating, collaborating, troubleshooting, helping, evaluating media, and trying again. In my classroom, I will design activities with multiple entry points and multiple ways to show understanding, especially for students who are multilingual, neurodivergent, disabled, chronically ill, culturally diverse, or different from the ``default'' learner schools often imagine.

This also changes how I think about assessment. I want to document literacy through observation, photos of student products, peer explanations, teacher notes, student talk, and moments of revision. A written response can still matter, but it should not be the only proof that learning happened. Especially in technology-rich or maker-based lessons, students often show understanding through action before they can fully explain it in writing.

In practice, I will continue designing projects where students create something meaningful and share it with an authentic audience. Students could build Minecraft worlds connected to science, create bilingual or multimodal explanations, make technology tutorials for peers, or build circuits and explain them through speaking, drawing, writing, or video. I will also make peer teaching part of the classroom culture because students often understand each other's barriers in ways adults can miss.

My biggest takeaway is that joy-oriented literacy is part of access. Through my \textit{Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS lens}, the question is not whether a task looks traditional enough to count as literacy. The question is whether the task gives students real access to meaning, identity, criticality, collaboration, and agency. When students feel capable, connected, and allowed to participate as themselves, literacy becomes a tool for liberation, self-determination, and empowerment.
