## Context

A Grade 4 workshop using Minecraft Education. Students were solving a timed dual-plate activation puzzle: their character had to stand on one gold plate while their coded agent reached the other, all within twenty seconds. Most students were stuck on direction logic and step-counting. Piter circulated, coached one-on-one, and redirected a finished student into a peer-teaching role before the class moved on to turtle and tree-trunk puzzles.

---

## Moment 1 — Debugging and Persistence: The Gold Plate Puzzle

*Supports: Example 1 — Debugging as Joyful Persistence*

A student attempts the dual-plate puzzle repeatedly. Piter sits with them to count steps and debug direction logic.

**Piter:** You can make it go this way, all the way here. You can count: one, two, three, four. And then you can make it go all the way straight to the golden. But you need to count how many steps. You're gonna make him turn right first. And then you're gonna make him go forward. How many steps? One, two, three, four — because the whole thing is right there. No, because he's already standing there, so you don't count that one. So it's one, two, three and four.

**Piter:** Let's test the logic first. Turn right, go forward four times.

**Piter:** So turn right — and go. Give me thirty seconds. One, two, three, four, right? And then you make it turn left, right? Now how many steps does it need for the golden one? That's what you need to count out. Let's count this first.

*Counting together:*

**Piter:** Count with me. The one... four, five, six. So we have six so far. That's assuming that your bot can float. Ten. Is ten in total? So try it out.

*Student tests the code. It fails. Piter debugs with them:*

**Piter:** So before that we say it's gonna turn right — but it needs to turn left. Right — okay. I think it was left, because it already turned right. So now I'll go back first to get this first one. You might have to reset. Where's your agent? There you go. Go all the way back so you can see him moving.

*Student tests again. After another reset and adjustment:*

**Piter:** Reset. Only one, that's it. Now there's three. One — take your time. Don't rush. Take your time. There you go. That's it — should open now. You need to stand there with the —

> *"You guys got it."*

## Moment 2 — Peer Teaching: Redirecting a Finished Student

*Supports: Example 2 — Peer Teaching as Joyful Expertise*

A student finishes the puzzle ahead of the group. Piter notices other students are far ahead and not sharing.

**Piter:** How come you guys are not giving him pointers? You guys are so far.

*Later, after a disruption at a different table, Piter redirects the advanced student:*

**Piter:** He's gonna help you. Come here. Go. Actually, you're too smart for this class. You need to help her. He's gonna help you. That's his job to help you.

> *"Nice, you guys okay perfect."*

*The redirected student moves to the peer's screen, diagnoses the issue, and guides them step by step. The peer completes the puzzle.*

**Piter** *(to the helper)***:** Help her please. Hold on, let me help somebody that is behind.

**Piter** *(to a different struggling student)***:** Do you want me to give you some pointers? This is what most of your classmates are doing. You can make your agent float. Turn this way, go forward, and then turn that way and go forward. How many steps do you have to go to the golden one? Count that with me.

*Once the peer finishes with the helper's guidance:*

> *"Out of here you go, baby!"*

> *"You did a good job."*

## Moment 3 — Collaborative Counting: Mathematical Literacy

*Supports: Example 3 — Collaborative Counting as Mathematical Literacy*

Piter sits with a student who cannot determine how many steps the agent needs. They count blocks out loud together.

**Piter:** So, count with me.

*Together, counting grid squares:*

**Piter:** Four, five, six. So we have six so far. That's assuming that your bot can float. Ten. Is ten in total? So try it out.

## Moment 4 — Student-Led Problem Framing: Reading the Trunk Puzzle

*Supports: Example 4 — Student-Led Problem Framing*

A student reaches a new puzzle level involving a tree trunk blocking the agent's path. Piter reads the instructions with the student.

**Piter:** So did you read the instructions? Because I think it wants you to do something. It says: use the agent to destroy the tree trunk that is in their way.

*The student reads the Minecraft environment and narrates the problem out loud:*

> *"It's this — it's all of this, and it goes up all the way up there because I need to go through there. That is a trunk?"*

**Piter:** I think the tree trunk is the wood stuff in there.
